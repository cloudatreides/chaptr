## 1. K-pop Romance Tropes: What Actually Gets Engagement

### Tier 1: Highest Engagement Tropes (Build These First)

**Trainee Survival Show / Debut Pressure**
The single most structurally rich trope in the space. Wattpad tags `#kpoptrainee`, `#traineelife`, and `#kpopgroup` each have hundreds of active stories, with survival show applyfics regularly hitting top-10 rankings in the K-pop category. The reason it works: it gives readers a built-in ticking clock (debut = deadline), a high-stakes environment where relationships are forbidden by the agency, and a clear underdog arc. Stories where the trainee protagonist must choose between the relationship and the debut slot consistently outperform in comments and votes.

Specific sub-tropes that compound engagement:
- The "dating ban" as external pressure (agency rules > personal choice = classic forbidden structure)
- Survival show elimination arcs (reader knows some trainees won't debut — creates dread)
- Shared dorm / proximity trope (forced closeness under surveillance)

**Enemies to Lovers**
Dominant across all of fanfiction, not just K-pop. On AO3, the enemies-to-lovers tag is one of the highest-volume romance tags across all fandoms. In K-pop fic specifically it manifests as: rival trainee, competitive castmate, or cold/arrogant senior idol who initially dismisses the protagonist. This trope is consistently the highest-engagement pairing structure in Wattpad K-pop categories. It also creates the most shareable "turning point" moments — readers screenshot or quote the exact chapter where the rival becomes the love interest.

**Forbidden Romance / Dating Ban**
Functionally inseparable from the trainee trope. The "idol dating ban" is culturally authentic to K-pop (real companies enforce these) which gives stories credibility. The forbidden structure forces every interaction to be charged. Even mundane scenes (practice room, hallway, elevator) carry weight because the relationship cannot exist publicly. This trope scales well into branching: "do you meet him secretly" vs "keep distance" creates genuinely different emotional trajectories.

### Tier 2: Strong Secondary Tropes (Use as Complications, Not Main Engine)

**Love Triangle / Second Lead Syndrome**
Heavy on Wattpad and TikTok as a discussion topic. Readers argue passionately about "Team A vs Team B" — which is social media gold. The risk: if the triangle resolves too obviously early, readers disengage. Best use in Chaptr is as a phase 2–3 mechanic where an early choice in chapter 1–2 determines which love interest becomes available in later chapters, rather than running both simultaneously throughout.

On TikTok, "second lead syndrome" content (readers upset that the 'wrong' guy won) generates enormous comment volume. This is a designed virality opportunity: two distinct endings for two lead characters, and let the community debate.

**Sasaeng / Obsessive Fan as Antagonist**
Medium engagement as a primary trope, high engagement as a complication. Works best as external threat rather than main romantic plot: a sasaeng targeting the male lead forces the protagonist into his orbit, creates danger, and justifies proximity. On Wattpad, stories that use sasaeng as a thriller element alongside romance outperform pure sasaeng-focused narratives.

**Transfer / New Student / Outsider**
The name of your story ("The Seoul Transfer") already captures this correctly. The outsider protagonist who doesn't know the social rules is the cleanest reader-insert vehicle because:
- Reader ignorance = character ignorance = no information asymmetry problem
- Outsider status justifies why the famous trainee notices this particular person
- "Fish out of water in Seoul / K-pop world" is relatable to your Western/international audience

### Tier 3: Handle Carefully

**Explicit idol fame / real-industry references**
The closer you get to real companies, shows, or procedures, the more the IP risk compounds. Use structurally accurate world-building but fictional scaffolding (see Section 3).

**Explicit mature content**
Your target is 13–22. Keep romantic tension high, physical escalation minimal (kissing ceiling, not beyond). This is also App Store / Play Store safe harbor.

### Trope Priority Recommendation for "The Seoul Transfer"

Primary engine: Transfer student (outsider) + Trainee life + Dating ban (forbidden)
Complication layer: Enemies-to-lovers arc with the cold main lead
Branching mechanic: Love triangle as chapter-unlock divergence (not running parallel)
External threat: Sasaeng as Act 2 antagonist

## 2. Chapter Structure: How Episode and Comparable Apps Work

### The Platform Standard (MEDIUM confidence — synthesized from Episode forums + Choice of Games community data)

**Minimum viable chapter:** 400 script lines in Episode's format. This translates to roughly 1,200–1,800 words of actual readable prose when you strip animation/direction commands.

**Recommended sweet spot:** 1,500–2,500 words of narrative text per chapter for a mobile K-pop romance. This maps to:
- 8–12 minutes of read time at average mobile reading pace (~200 words/minute)
- 3–5 meaningful choices within that window
- One "cliffhanger" or emotionally charged beat at the end

**Session length target:** Aim for 10–15 minutes per session. Mobile interactive story players typically complete 1–2 chapters per session. Design each chapter to function as a complete emotional unit (setup → tension → choice → partial resolution → hook to next chapter).

**Choice frequency rule:** The Choice of Games community standard is "first choice within 300 words." For a romance app with younger readers, the first choice should appear before the end of the first scene — ideally within the first 250–400 words. Readers who make a choice in the first minute are significantly more likely to continue.

**Choices per chapter breakdown:**
- Chapter 1 (hook): 2–3 choices. Low-stakes. Establish personality. ("How do you respond to the rude comment?" — sets tone, not outcome)
- Chapters 2–5 (rising action): 3–5 choices. Mix of cosmetic (dialogue style) and branching (path-affecting)
- Chapter 6+ (escalation): 4–6 choices. At least 2 should feel genuinely consequential
- "Boss" chapter (turning point): 1 high-stakes binary choice. Maximum weight. This is your screenshot moment.

**Do not:**
- Open chapter 1 with character customization (Episode internal guidance explicitly flags this as a drop-off risk)
- Run more than 600 words between any two choices in chapters 2+
- End a chapter mid-choice (platform standard — always resolve the choice before chapter end)

**Chapter structure template for Chaptr:**

```
[SCENE OPEN — 150–200 words, establish setting and mood]
[FIRST CHOICE — within first 300 words, low stakes]
[SCENE CONTINUE — 300–500 words, character interaction]
[SECOND CHOICE — relationship-defining or path-affecting]
[SCENE ESCALATION — 400–600 words, tension rises]
[THIRD CHOICE — optional: personality expression OR skip if pacing demands]
[CHAPTER CLIMAX — 200–300 words, emotional peak]
[CLIFFHANGER / HOOK — 50–100 words, sets next chapter]
```

Total: ~1,500–2,000 words. 3 choices minimum. 5 choices maximum.

## 3. Original Group Naming and World-Building Conventions

### The Core IP Avoidance Strategy

Never: real company names (HYBE, SM, JYP, YG), real group names, real idol names, real show names (Produce 101, Mnet)
Always: structurally accurate, culturally authentic fictional equivalents

This is exactly how K-drama productions handle it. They reference "a major entertainment company in Apgujeong" rather than "SM Entertainment in Apgujeong." The setting feels real; the legal exposure disappears.

### Agency Naming Patterns That Work

Fan authors consistently use these construction patterns (observed across Wattpad/AO3 original character fics):

**Pattern 1: Initials + Entertainment**
Examples: SH Entertainment, KM Entertainment, NOVA Entertainment
Why it works: Mirrors real naming conventions exactly. Readers fill in the implied real referent without you stating it.

**Pattern 2: Concept/Aspiration word + Entertainment/Company**
Examples: Apex Entertainment, Lunar Entertainment, Horizon Entertainment, Starfield Company
Why it works: Sounds credible, implies scale, avoids any specific real-world association.

**Pattern 3: Korean word transliterated + Entertainment**
Examples: Hanguk Entertainment, Mirae (Future) Entertainment, Seum Entertainment
Why it works: Authentically Korean without mapping to any real entity.

**Recommendation for Chaptr:** Use "NOVA Entertainment" or "Apex Entertainment" as the fictional agency in "The Seoul Transfer." Short, authoritative, no real-world collision, easy to hashtag.

### Group Naming Conventions

Real K-pop group names follow consistent patterns you can replicate:

- **All-caps acronym with meaning:** BTS (Behind the Scene), EXO, ATEEZ, TWICE
- **English concept word:** Stray Kids, TOMORROW X TOGETHER, BLACKPINK
- **Korean + English hybrid:** SHINee, GOT7, ENHYPEN
- **Numbers with meaning:** 2PM, 4MINUTE, Wanna One

For Chaptr's male lead group, use an acronym structure. Something like:

**VEIL** (Valid / Elite / Idol / Life — acronym meanings can be retrofitted)
**NEXUS** (implies connection, multiple members)
**DAWN** or **DUSK** (concept-forward, easy fandom name derivation)
**SERO** (invented word, Korean vowel sounds, no existing group collision)

Avoid: Any name that too closely phonetically resembles an existing group.

### Trainee System World-Building Beats

These are the structural elements readers expect and that make the world feel authentic:

- Trainees sign with the agency at 14–18 (sometimes younger)
- Monthly evaluations determine who stays / gets cut
- Strict dormitory living with curfews and phone restrictions
- Dating is explicitly prohibited by contract (the "dating ban")
- Debut slots are finite — the group lineup isn't final until an announcement
- The survival show mechanism: sometimes the public votes on the final lineup (huge stakes)
- Sunbae/hoobae hierarchy (senior/junior dynamic within the agency)
- Satoori (regional dialect) as a character trait — common in fics to mark the "outsider" protagonist

For "The Seoul Transfer" specifically: the protagonist arriving mid-training cycle is realistic (agencies do accept transfer trainees, especially with sought-after skills). This also explains why she's an outsider to established social dynamics.

## 4. Claude Prompt Engineering for Narrative Consistency

### The Core Problem

LLM-generated interactive fiction fails in three predictable ways:
1. Character voice drift (character sounds different chapter to chapter)
2. State amnesia (Claude forgets a choice made 3 chapters ago)
3. Generic AI register (prose sounds like a content summary, not a story)

All three are solvable with disciplined prompt architecture.

### System Prompt Structure

Every generation call should include a system prompt with these sections:

**Section 1: Character Bible (static, never changes)**
```
CHARACTER: Han Jiwoo (male lead)
- Voice: terse, sarcastic in group settings; quietly intense in private
- Speech pattern: short sentences. Rarely asks questions. States observations.
- Never says: "I feel", "I think", "Maybe we should"
- Always says: direct imperatives, blunt observations
- Physical tell: jaw tightens when frustrated, doesn't look at people he's avoiding
- Core wound: previous partner was only interested in his debut prospects
- What he wants: to debut without owing anyone anything
- What he actually needs: someone who sees him before the fame
```

Provide this level of specificity for every named character. Vague character bibles produce vague character voices.

**Section 2: Story State Block (dynamic, updated each chapter)**
```
CURRENT STORY STATE:
- Chapter: 4
- User name: [NAME]
- Affection meter: Jiwoo +2, Minjun +1
- Choices made:
  * Ch1: Defended Jiwoo against agency criticism (Jiwoo +1, trust flag: set)
  * Ch2: Chose to practice alone instead of joining group session (independence flag: set)
  * Ch3: Helped Minjun with choreography (Minjun +1)
- Active flags: trust_jiwoo, independent_protagonist
- Pending threads: Jiwoo saw protagonist's notebook contents (unresolved)
- Agency knows about: nothing yet
```

This state block is your continuity layer. Every chapter output must reference it and every chapter completion must produce an updated version.

**Section 3: Prose Style Constraints**
```
WRITING STYLE:
- POV: Second person ("You walk into the practice room")
- Tense: Present
- Sentence rhythm: Vary length. Short sentences for tension. Longer for atmosphere.
- Avoid: adverb stacking, "suddenly", "she/he couldn't help but", thought-reporting instead of showing
- Target register: Young adult commercial fiction, not literary. Think Anna Todd's After or Jenny Han's To All the Boys.
- Emotional specificity: Name exact physical sensations, not abstract emotions ("your chest tightens" not "you feel nervous")
```

**Section 4: This Chapter's Constraints**
```
THIS CHAPTER:
- Scene: Dance studio, 11pm, only you and Jiwoo remain
- Goal: First moment of genuine vulnerability from Jiwoo
- Required choice at ~400 words: [Player decides whether to ask about the scar on his wrist or stay silent]
- Required choice at ~900 words: [Player decides whether to leave when agency supervisor checks in or hide]
- End beat: Jiwoo says something uncharacteristically honest, then immediately retreats
- Do NOT resolve: What the scar means (save for Chapter 7)
- Word count target: 1,600–1,900 words
```

### Anti-Drift Techniques

**Few-shot anchoring:** Include 2–3 paragraphs of your own authored "gold standard" prose in the system prompt as style reference. Tell Claude explicitly: "Match this voice and register." This is the single most effective technique for preventing generic AI output.

**Recap injection:** Before each new chapter generation, feed Claude a 200-word summary of all previous chapters written in the same second-person voice. This anchors the model in the established narrative timeline.

**Character voice checks:** After generating a chapter, run a follow-up prompt: "List every line of Jiwoo's dialogue in this chapter. Does any line contradict his established voice pattern? If yes, rewrite those lines only." This is faster than regenerating the full chapter.

**Prohibited phrases list:** Explicitly list phrases that signal AI register and ban them in the system prompt. Common offenders: "little did she know", "her heart skipped a beat", "butterflies in her stomach", "he/she couldn't help but notice", "suddenly", "in that moment". Replace with physical specificity.

### Branching State Management Architecture

Do not rely on Claude to track state in conversational memory. Claude's context window is large but state drift is real in long sessions.

Recommended architecture:
1. Maintain a `story_state.json` object in your app database
2. Each chapter completion writes the player's choices and any flag changes to this object
3. Each generation request pulls the current state object and injects it into the system prompt as structured text
4. Claude reads the state, generates accordingly, and the app parses the output for any state changes

This means Claude's job is purely generative (write this scene given this context), not archival (remember what happened). The app handles memory; Claude handles prose.

## 5. Second-Person "You Are the MC" Framing Conventions

### YN / Reader-Insert Standards

The Y/N (Your Name) convention originated on Tumblr and migrated to Wattpad and AO3. Current best practices from the writing community:

**Name insertion:** Use `[Name]` as placeholder in generated text. Your app replaces this on render. More natural than `Y/N` in a designed app experience (Y/N reads as fan fiction shorthand; `[Name]` renders invisibly).

**Pronoun handling:** The community has evolved. Best practice in 2024–2025 is to default to "you/your" (second person) and avoid gendered pronouns for the protagonist entirely. This works naturally in second person and makes the story feel inclusive without requiring explicit pronoun selection prompts that break immersion.

**Physical description:** The protagonist should have minimal locked physical attributes. Let the selfie upload handle visual identity. In prose, reference what others observe about you indirectly: "He keeps looking at you" rather than "He noticed your [hair color] hair." If you do anchor a physical detail, use something the player chose (outfit choice, hairstyle from an early chapter selection).

**Personality via choices:** The reader-insert protagonist should not have a preset personality in the prose. Her personality is constructed by the reader through choices. Avoid lines like "You're not the type to back down" — those assert a trait the player may not have chosen. Instead, let the choices express personality: if the player chose confrontation in chapter 1, Jiwoo can reference it later ("You're the one who said what everyone was thinking").

**Voice in second person:** Present tense second person is the standard for this genre. "You step into the practice room and the bass from the speakers hits your chest before you hear it." Past tense second person exists but feels more literary and less immediate — not right for this audience.

**Avoiding the hollow protagonist problem:** The single biggest failure mode in Y/N fic is a protagonist with no interiority. She needs inner voice, observations, humor, contradictions. The choices reveal her values; the narration reveals her perception. "You watch him and think: he moves like he's the only person in the room. You hate that you notice."

### Selfie / Visual Protagonist Integration

Since users upload a selfie as their visual identity:
- Reference the protagonist visually only when the narrative justifies it (mirror scene, another character's reaction)
- Never describe her face in prose — the game image handles that
- Use clothing choices, movement, and reactions to establish visual presence instead
- When the male lead notices her, have him react to something non-facial: "the way you stopped mid-step", "the notebook you're always carrying"

## 6. TikTok Virality Strategy for Interactive Story Content

### What Actually Goes Viral (Evidence-Based)

BookTok's proven formats directly applicable to Chaptr:

**Format 1: "The Choice That Broke Me" reaction video**
Creator plays through to a high-stakes choice, films their genuine reaction, shows the outcome. Template: face-cam overlay on the app screen. Audio: trending sound or dramatic music. Caption: "i did NOT expect this." This format works because it documents authentic emotional response and makes viewers want to experience the same moment themselves.

Action for Chaptr: Design at least one chapter per story arc specifically as a "screenshot moment" — a choice so devastating or charged that players will want to document their reaction. The binary choice structure (Chapter 6+ "boss choice") is your engineered virality point.

**Format 2: "Team Jiwoo vs Team [Second Lead]" debate content**
The love triangle creates a built-in debate structure. Post a question: "who did you choose and why" — let comments war. This is not passive content; it requires the two leads to be genuinely compelling alternatives, not an obvious choice.

Action for Chaptr: The second lead (e.g., Minjun) cannot be a narrative consolation prize. He needs his own fans. Give him a story beat in chapter 3–4 that makes him the obvious right choice — then give Jiwoo a chapter 5 beat that reverses that. The tension between the two needs to be unresolved enough for genuine debate.

**Format 3: "My ending vs your ending" split-screen**
Two creators compare their story outcomes side by side. This requires meaningfully different endings to exist. One player ends up debuting alongside Jiwoo; another ends with a clean break and a solo path. The endings must be distinct enough to be worth comparing.

Action for Chaptr: At minimum, design 3 distinct chapter 10 endings (happy route, bittersweet route, protagonist-chooses-herself route). Each is shareable and the divergence itself is discussion content.

**Format 4: "The lore drop" — story world detail videos**
Creators explain fictional world details they found compelling. "The trainee contract in Chaptr has an actual clause that says..." — builds world investment, makes the story feel researched and real. This is the sleeper format: low-production, high-rewatch.

Action for Chaptr: Build 3–5 "lore artifacts" into the story world — a contract clause players encounter, a ranking chart from a fictional survival show, a screenshot of a fictional group chat. These become shareable TikTok content independent of the app.

**Format 5: "Replayability reveal"**
"I went back and chose differently and THIS is what happened" — shows dramatically different outcomes from a single choice. Requires real branching to work; the outcome difference must be visible and emotionally distinct, not just different dialogue.

Action for Chaptr: At least one "major branch" per story arc where the two paths feel genuinely different for 2+ chapters before reconverging, if they reconverge at all.

### The Structural Viral Ingredients

Every chapter should contain at least one of these:

1. **Quotable line** — a Jiwoo line that sounds like a book quote. Short, specific, emotionally precise. "You keep showing up in my practice hours like you're on the schedule." These become TikTok text overlays.

2. **Screenshot choice** — a choice framed as a moral dilemma, not a preference. "Do you tell the agency what you saw, knowing it could end his debut?" This is the choice players post about.

3. **Lore detail** — one world-building specific that makes readers feel they're inside a real industry. A training schedule, a real Korean music industry reference, an agency rumor. Grounds the story.

4. **Reversal beat** — one moment per chapter that subverts what the reader expected. Jiwoo does something kind when he's been cold. The agency rules bend for someone unexpected. The reversal is the most-commented beat.

### TikTok Content Calendar for Launch

Pre-launch (weeks before app release):
- Character introduction videos: "Meet Jiwoo. He's the problem." Fictional character cards with aesthetic visuals
- World-building teaser: "What is NOVA Entertainment's training contract?" — post the fictional contract as a prop
- Casting call: "What does [Name] look like to you?" — use user-submitted selfies to tease the customization feature

Launch week:
- "First chapter reaction" — show chapter 1 on screen, react live or pre-recorded
- "The choice I already regret" — show the first major choice with dramatic energy
- Duet/stitch format: "Tell me your chapter 3 choice and I'll tell you your ending"

Post-launch (ongoing):
- Weekly "route reveals" — show what the other path looked like
- Community debate posts: "Team Jiwoo or Team Minjun — comment your choice and why"
- Creator reactions to player choices: "You all chose WHAT in chapter 6??"

## Synthesis: Content Decisions for "The Seoul Transfer"

### Commit to These

1. **Trainee + forbidden = your primary emotional engine.** The dating ban is not a background detail — it is the structural reason every romantic beat has stakes. Make it present in every chapter: a rule being bent, a risk being taken, or a moment being stolen.

2. **Design chapter 6 as your engineered viral moment.** One binary choice, maximum stakes (debut vs relationship, or loyalty vs honesty), no obviously right answer. This is the screenshot and reaction content moment.

3. **Give the second lead (Minjun) a genuine fan base by chapter 4.** He needs a scene where he is unambiguously the better choice. Then Jiwoo gets chapter 5 to reclaim ground. The debate between them is your community content.

4. **Use NOVA Entertainment as the fictional agency.** Clean, authoritative, no IP collision.

5. **Use `[Name]` placeholder, present-tense second person, no locked physical protagonist traits in prose.** Let the selfie handle visual identity; let the choices build personality.

6. **System prompt architecture:** character bible + state block + style constraints + chapter brief. Never rely on Claude's conversational memory for state. Your app owns the state object.

7. **Three distinct endings minimum.** Happy, bittersweet, protagonist-chooses-herself. Each is TikTok content.

### Avoid These

- Real company / group names anywhere in the story text (even as background cultural reference)
- A second lead who is clearly a consolation prize (kills debate virality)
- Chapters longer than 2,500 words (session drop-off risk)
- More than 600 words between choices (reader disengagement)
- Prose that describes the protagonist's face or locks her physical traits
- Choice framing that feels like preference (flavor) rather than value (consequence)
